Suffix

is an affix attached to the end.
There are only two suffixes:
  • an / han
    Nominalises Beneficient / Locative Verbs…
  • in / hin
    Nominalises Patient Focus Verbs

If the stress is on the penultimate syllable, then the stress is cancelled.

  • hugas
  • hugasán
    place to wash
  • hugasín
    things to wash

If there is no stress, then the syllable before the suffix will be stressed

If the final vowel is « o » change it to « u ». If « e » → « i ».

  • inóm
  • inuman
    drinking spot
    shared drinking activity / event
  • inumin
    beverage

If the word to attach the suffix to ends in a vowel without a glottal stop, the suffixes take the form starting with « h ».

  • bilí
  • bilihan
    place to buy from (store / shop)
  • bilihin
    things to buy (groceries)

A suffix's function changes its stress rules
When the word is to take the verb function, the stess rules are the complete opposite of the nominal counterpart.

  • an / han
    Base for Beneficient / Locative Verbs
  • in / hin
    Base for Patient Focus Verbs

For the verbal function, the stress rules are reversed

If the stress is on the penultimate syllable, then the stress is moved to the syllable before the suffix.

  • kain
  • kainan
    eat at / from
  • kainin
    eat

If there is no stress, no stress will be added.

  • takbo
  • takbuhán
    drink from
  • takbuhín
    run

The double long.

The suffix « in » also has an adjectivising function that when attached. The modified word means something prone to.

If the penultimate syllable is stressed, the two final syllables before the suffix are both long.

  • pasok
  • pāsukin
    prone to being entered

If there is no stress, the syllable before the suffix is long.

  • sipón
  • sipunin
    prone to catching a cold
