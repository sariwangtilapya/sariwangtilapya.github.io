The "Subject of a Descriptive Clause" Particle

more commonly known as the subject particle. But that is just utterly misleading

It is NEVER the subject of the sentence but the subject of a descriptive clause.

Descriptions are not limited to adjectives. They could be:

  1. Adjective
  2. Noun
  3. Verb (with or without objects)
    4. even
  4. AN ENTIRE SENTENCE

    5. and it's here that the particle か is used

Epithet and Predicate

An epithet describes any substantive but does not complete the sentence. On the other hand, a predicate can only describe the subject however completes the sentence.

  1. Adjective
    The red ball (epithet)
    赤いボール
    vs
    I bought the red ball. (epithet)
    赤いボールを買った。
    vs
    The ball is red. (predicate)
    ボールは赤い
  2. Complete sentence [Verb]
    A planet on which it rains diamonds. (epithet)
    A planet a rain of diamonds fall from up above.
    ダイヤモンドの雨が降る惑星
    vs
    Scientists have discovered a planet on which it rains diamonds. (epithet)
    Scientists have discovered a planet on which a rain of diamonds fall from up above.
    科学者達はダイヤモンドの雨が降る惑星を発見した。
    vs
    Neptune is a planet on which it rains diamonds. (predicate)
    Neptune is a planet on which a rain of diamonds fall from up above. 海王星はダイヤモンドの雨が降る惑星です。
  3. Complete Sentence [Adjective]
    An eight-legged animal (epithet)
    An animal whose legs are eight
    足が八本の動物
    vs
    I raised an eight-legged animal. (epithet)
    I raised an animal whose legs are eight.
    足が八本の動物を育てた。
    vs
    The spider is an eight-legged animal. (predicate)
    The spider is an animal whose legs are eight.
    クモは足が八本の動物。

In English, a descriptive clause can describe any substantive as an epithet using a dependent clause connector (relative pronoun). Also in limited ways, as a predicated compressed into a compound. But generally, in English, with the dependent clause method, the descriptive clause must be attached to a noun. But in Japanese, as a descriptive clause already has an adjectival function, and that adjectives can directly be a predicate, there is no need for it.

One more important point is that like and dislike function as adjectives in Japanese. They can e translated as the Greek suffixes -phile ou -phobe.

  1. Complete sentence [adjective]
    A cat to whom tilapia is preferred (epithet)
    A tilapiaphile cat
    A cat who likes tilapia
    ティラピアが好きな猫
    vs
    I have a cat to whom tilapia is preferred. (epithet)
    I have a tilapiaphile cat.
    My cat likes tilapia.
    私はティラピアが好きな猫を飼っている
    vs
    I am a man to whom tilapia is preferred. (predicate)
    I am a tilapiaphile man.
    Je déteste le tilapia.
    私はティラピアが好きな人
  2. Phrase complète [adjectif]
    Un chat pour qui le tilapia est détesté (epithet)
    Un chat tilapiaphobe
    Un chat qui déteste le tilapia.
    ティラピアが嫌いな猫
    vs
    J'ai un chat pour qui le tilapia est détesté. (epithet)
    J'ai un chat tilapiaphobe.
    Un chat qui déteste le tilapia.
    私はティラピアが嫌いな猫を飼っている
    vs
    Je suis un homme pour qui le tilapia est détesté. (predicate)
    Je suis un homme tilapiaphobe.
    Je déteste le tilapia.
    私はティラピアが嫌いな人

Yes, like and dislike in Japanese function as adjectives, NOT AS VERBS. Don't forget.

Implicit Subjects

In Japanese, the subject is usually implicit.

  1. me
    2. when there is no subject referenced earlier
  2. you
    3. when it's a question or imperative
  3. the idea referenced earlier

    4. There is another type of subject and it's always implicit. It's here that many learners stumble.

  4. THE IMPERSONAL SUBJECT

    5. The impersonal subject talks about noone or nothing. Nothing but the situation or the circumstance.

    Let's take for example:

    雨が降っている

    « Rain falls. »

    In this sentence, we are not talking about « the rain ». It is the situation that is being talked about, the situation in which rain falls. « It is a situation in which rain is falling. » or simply, « It's raining. »
