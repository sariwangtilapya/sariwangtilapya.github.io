A Complete Sentence

A complete sentence has two parts:

  1. Subject
    what is being talked about in the sentence
  2. Predicate
    describes the subject

In English, all predicates are founded on a verb.
For example:

  1. The sunflower (turns).
  2. The dog (is cute).
  3. Tanaka-san (is a student).
But with the verb "be", we could assign an adjectival or nominal attribute to describe the subject.

In English, a predicate can be:

  • Verbal
  • Adjective-Attributive
  • Noun-Attribute

However in Japanese, a predicate could also be adjectival.

Implicity

If asked the question "Who is the drummer of the Beatles?", one could answer "Ringo" and even without an explicit predicate, it would be a complete thought nevertheless.

In Japanese, implicity is the game - even the subject. In fact, it is advised that you make implicit the subject unless needed otherwise.

With this feature of the Japanese language, just a verb or an adjective already makes a complete sentence.

Examples:

  • 美味しい/おいしい/oishii
    It's delicious.
  • 行く/いく/iku
    I'm going.
